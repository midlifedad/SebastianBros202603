# THE WRONG RUBRIC
## A Presentation Narrative for Amir Haque
### 2026 FinMa Family Reunion — Westin Resort & Spa, Whistler BC
### Thursday, March 12, 2026 | ~45 minutes

---

*This document is a narrative guide — not a script and not a slide deck. It describes what happens in each moment of the talk, why it works for this specific audience, and offers suggested language Amir can absorb and then deliver in his own voice. The quoted passages are starting points, not teleprompter copy. Internalize the intent behind each beat, then say it the way it comes out of your mouth.*

## BEFORE YOU BEGIN: THE ROOM

By the time you stand up, this audience has been sitting through presentations for a day and a half. Wednesday was wall-to-wall 45-minute company updates. Thursday morning, they heard Benjamin Kromayer on AI process automation for irrigation — tactical, grounded, operational. Then Wali Mahmood on preparing businesses for investment — financial, deal-centric. Both valuable. Both left-brain.

The audience is full but their attention is being rationed. They're thinking about lunch. Some are thinking about the afternoon pitch competition. A few are still processing what they heard earlier.

Your job in the first ninety seconds is to signal: **this is going to be different.** Not louder. Not more polished. Just different in texture. You're shifting the room from receiving information to *experiencing* something.

The room is small — twenty to twenty-five people. This isn't a stage. It's a living room. Make eye contact. Use names when it feels natural. Let pauses do work. The intimacy is your advantage.

## SECTION 1: THE WRONG RUBRIC
**Target: ~7 minutes | Energy: Confident, slightly provocative, then interactive and warm**

### The Opening — Steve Jobs, 1983

**ON SCREEN:** The Steve Jobs video clip from 1983 — the passage about how every new medium initially imitates the medium that preceded it. Television was radio with pictures. Computers were fancy calculators. Each medium had to discover its own native language.

*Let the clip play. Don't talk over it. Let Jobs do the work. The audience will lean in because it's unexpected — they didn't expect a 43-year-old video clip. The grainy quality, the young Jobs, the prescience — all of it creates a moment.*

**[CLIP ENDS]**

**[BEAT — one full second of silence before you speak]**

Suggested language:

> "That's from 1983. Forty-three years ago. And it perfectly describes what's happening with AI right now."

*Pause. Let it connect.*

> "Most people — most businesses — are using AI as a faster Google. A better autocomplete. A cheaper freelancer. They're pointing cameras at a radio show and calling it television."

> "That's the wrong rubric."

*This framing does two things. First, it tells the room that whatever they think they know about AI, you're about to reframe it. Second, it gives them permission to set aside their current mental model without feeling attacked. You're not saying they're wrong — you're saying the *rubric* is wrong. It's a subtle but important distinction, especially for a room with strong egos and real expertise.*

### The Twist Jobs Couldn't Predict

> "But here's what Jobs couldn't have predicted. With every previous technology shift, you had time. TV took *decades* to develop its own language. Film took decades. The internet took at least a decade before it stopped trying to be a digital newspaper."

> "With AI, you don't get decades. You don't even get years. The medium reinvents its own language every few months. By the time you figure out the right rubric, the rubric has already changed."

> "Let me show you what I mean."

*This is where you make it concrete and personal.*

### The Preparation Evolution

**ON SCREEN:** A simple visual progression — four stages. These can be four panels, or a simple animation that builds. Keep it clean and easy to read at a glance.

> "Here's how I prepared for this presentation."

Walk through each stage conversationally. Don't read from screen — glance at it, then talk to the room:

> "The old way — and some of you have seen me present before — I'd Google your names, skim some LinkedIn profiles, pull together some generic slides, hope for the best."

> "A couple years ago, I started using ChatGPT to help. Feed it my notes, have it help shape the deck. Faster. Still basically the same process with an AI assistant."

> "Last year, it got more sophisticated. I'd feed it prior presentations, the event agenda, research on the audience — and it would help me build a narrative arc tailored to the room."

> "This time..."

*Slow down here. This is the turn.*

> "This time, I used a tool called Perplexity Computer — it launched a few days ago — to deep-research every person in this room. Your backgrounds. Your companies. The challenges you're navigating. I had it analyze the full agenda so I'd know exactly what you've already heard by the time I stand up. And then I built a custom companion application — just for this talk. It's disposable. It exists only for this hour. After today, it's gone."

*Let that register. Some people will look skeptical. Some will look intrigued. Both reactions are good.*

> "And here's the thing that should bother you a little: if I give this same talk next month, I'll prepare for it in a way I literally cannot describe to you right now. Because the tools will be different. The medium will have changed again."

*This is the "wrong rubric" thesis made tangible. You're not speaking in abstractions — you're showing them that your own process has changed four times in three years, and the pace is accelerating.*

### The Interactive Moment — QR Code

**[ENERGY SHIFT — warmer, lighter, almost playful]**

> "So let me show you. Take out your phones."

**ON SCREEN:** A QR code. Large. Clean. Nothing else on the slide.

*Give people a moment to pull out their phones. Don't rush this. If someone is fumbling, that's fine — the room fills the space with its own energy.*

> "Scan that. Enter your name."

**As each person logs in, their portrait appears on the main screen — transformed into a creative visual theme.** *(The specific theme — Marvel characters, Renaissance portraits, whatever lands best — should be decided in advance and tested. The key is that it's personal, surprising, and a little bit delightful.)*

*The room will react. People will laugh, point, nudge each other. Let this breathe. React to what's happening. Use names:*

> "Dirk, that might be the best one yet."

> "Shane, I think you got the short end of the stick on that one."

*This isn't filler — this is the demonstration. You built something that personalizes in real-time for a room of twenty-five people. The audience is experiencing the thesis, not hearing about it.*

**On each person's phone:** A personalized relevance guide — their background, their company, and specific callouts for which parts of the upcoming presentation are most relevant to them and why.

*You don't need to explain everything that's on their phones. Let them discover it. Some will read it during the talk. That's fine — it means the content is working.*

**[SETTLE THE ROOM — bring the energy back to center]**

> "Everything you're looking at right now — the research on each of you, the app in your hand, the images on screen — that was one person. A few days."

*Pause.*

> "And honestly? If I'd started this a month later, it probably would have taken half the time."

*Pause again. Shorter this time.*

> "That's what I want to talk about today."

**WHY THIS WORKS FOR THIS AUDIENCE:** You've just shown, not told. The financial people (Shane, Wali, Stephen, Patrick) are already doing mental math on labor compression. The technical people (Troy, Benjamin, Charl) are wondering about the architecture. The operators (Martin, Mark Salter, Eric) are thinking about applications. And Dirk Sebastian — who watched a biotech company he helped build get acquired for €1.3 billion — recognizes the scent of exponential change. You've earned their attention for the next thirty-five minutes.

## SECTION 2: THE EXPONENTIAL ARC
**Target: ~15 minutes | Energy: Building from historical grounding to visceral proof to personal crescendo**

*This section follows one continuous arc — the exponential progression — and you ride it through three domains: creative output (images/video), business transformation (the agency), and software creation (coding). The audience should feel like they're accelerating along the curve with you, each phase more dramatic than the last. The resets you experienced aren't disconnected stories — they're waypoints on the same exponential, and the decreasing distance between them IS the point.*

### The Origin Point

> "August 14th, 2020. Ten-thirty in the morning. I type playground.openai.com into my browser and try GPT-3 for the first time."

*The specificity matters. You remember the date and the time. That signals this was a defining moment, not a casual experiment.*

> "Most people hadn't heard of it. 'GPT' wasn't even a word yet. But I sat there for four hours and I knew — I *knew* — that I was looking at something that was going to change everything I did for the rest of my life."

> "And I want to be clear: I didn't predict this because I'm especially prescient. I knew because it had already been predicted."

### The Singularity Grounding

*This is important — you're establishing that the exponential isn't a surprise. It was mapped. We're on schedule.*

> "Some of you may have heard of the concept of the technological singularity. The idea that computational power, and the intelligence built on top of it, follows an exponential curve — and that at some point, it crosses a threshold where the rate of change becomes so fast that it fundamentally transforms human civilization."

> "This was mapped out decades ago. The curve was drawn. The dates were projected. And when I sat there in August 2020 typing into GPT-3, I could see — we were right on the curve. Right where the projections said we'd be. This wasn't a surprise. It was the schedule."

> "What nobody could predict — what nobody *can* predict — is what the implications actually feel like when you're living inside them. The math was right. But math doesn't prepare you for what it's like when your entire understanding of what's possible breaks and reforms every few months."

*This framing is crucial for this audience. You're not a hype man. You're someone who understood the underlying model and has been tracking against it. The financial minds in the room (Shane, Wali, Stephen) think in curves and projections — you're speaking their language. And you're setting up the first principle: you can know the direction, but you can't predict the distance.*

### The Exponential Setup

> "Let me make this concrete. Quick show of hands — how many of you have heard the thing about linear steps versus exponential steps? Thirty linear steps gets you thirty meters, thirty exponential steps gets you around the Earth — that one?"

**[READ THE ROOM]**

- **If most hands go up:** "Good. So I'll skip the math. But what I want to do is show you what that abstraction actually *looks like* when it plays out in the real world. Because going around the earth is abstract. What I'm about to show you is concrete — and it's the thing that hit my business first."

- **If most hands stay down:** Take 60–90 seconds to walk through it simply. "Thirty steps linearly — one, two, three — you end up thirty meters away. Thirty steps exponentially — one, two, four, eight, sixteen — you end up a billion meters away. Twenty-six times around the world. The human brain is built for linear. We are *terrible* at intuiting exponential curves. And that matters, because you're living inside one right now."

*Either way, the bridge is the same: that was the abstract version. Now let me show you the real version — applied to the thing that affected my original business the most.*

### Phase One of the Arc: Creative Output (Images → Video)

> "My core business is advertising and creativity. So this is where I felt it first."

**ON SCREEN:** A sequence of AI-generated images — same prompt, generated across successive versions of Midjourney (or equivalent). V3 (2022), V4, V5, V6, and the current state-of-the-art (2026).

*Click through these deliberately. Don't rush. Let each one sit for two to three seconds before advancing. The progression speaks for itself — from crude and uncanny to photorealistic and expressive.*

> "Same prompt. Every few months. Watch what happens."

*As you click through, the room will react. The later images will draw audible responses. That's the point.*

> "We've gotten used to this. We take it for granted now. But stop and think about what just happened on this screen. In the span of about eighteen months, this went from a blurry, uncanny mess to something indistinguishable from professional photography. That's the exponential. And we've already normalized it."

> "I was off by ten years. In 2022, I saw V3 and I thought — maybe by 2032 this gets photorealistic. It took eighteen months. I knew the direction. I was wildly wrong about the distance."

*Now the video progression:*

**ON SCREEN:** A current-state AI-generated video — the most impressive, freshest example available. *(Update this right up until the day of the talk. Something that showcases cinematic quality, coherent motion, realistic physics. A Seed Dance video or whatever is most jaw-dropping at the time.)*

*Let the video play in full. Don't narrate over it.*

**[VIDEO ENDS]**

> "Videos landed roughly when I expected them to. But here's what that tells me — even when I *try* to calibrate for the exponential, even when I know the curve, I'm still going to be wrong about *something*. I got video right and images wildly wrong. The direction was clear. The distance was unknowable."

*This is the first principle planted: you can see the direction but never predict the distance. Don't label it yet.*

### Phase Two of the Arc: Business Transformation (The Agency)

*Now shift from what you can see to what it's done to your work. This should feel like a natural acceleration — same curve, new domain.*

> "So that's what the exponential looks like in creative output. Let me show you what it's done to my actual business."

> "I run an ad agency. Or at least that's what it says on the door."

> "We used to spend eight weeks on a brand strategy assignment. Research, customer interviews, desk research, competitive analysis — all feeding into a framework, a template, a presentation. Layers of process. And if I'm being honest, a lot of that process existed to build consensus. To make the client feel like the thinking was rigorous."

> "That entire process now takes about six minutes."

*Let the number land. Some people will think you're exaggerating. That's fine. The specificity — six minutes, not "much faster" — forces them to take it seriously.*

> "But here's the thing that took me a while to understand. That output — the brand strategy framework — used to be the *deliverable*. The thing we'd present. The thing the client paid for."

> "Now? It's just the starting input."

> "I can generate fifty of them and use each one as the starting point for campaign development, product strategy, five-year planning, market entry analysis. Yesterday's endpoint is today's starting line."

*This is the second principle, planted but not yet named: every output becomes the input for the next thing.*

*Now show them what that transformation actually produced — the products:*

> "And this isn't theoretical. Let me show you what we've built."

**ON SCREEN:** A brief visual or video demo of the AI focus group system — showing how it simulates consumer research with hundreds or thousands of synthetic respondents, testing positioning, messaging, product concepts.

> "We built an AI focus group system. I can test a brand positioning with a thousand simulated consumers in minutes. Different demographics, different psychographics, different markets. The kind of research that used to cost six figures and take two months — we can run it on demand, iterate in real time, and test variations we never would have had the budget to explore."

> "We haven't even tried to commercialize this yet. It's just... a thing we built because we needed it. And that's the part that should get your attention — the things that used to be entire businesses are becoming features. Things you build in a week because you need them, and you don't even bother to sell."

*That last line — "things that used to be entire businesses are becoming features" — is a devastatingly efficient summary of what's happening. Let it land.*

**WHY THIS LANDS FOR THIS ROOM:** Shane managed $6 billion in assets — he understands what it means when an eight-week process compresses to six minutes. Wali does M&A advisory — he's thinking about how this affects valuation models and what businesses are even worth acquiring. Emma runs tax advisory at MNP — she's thinking about what happens to billable hours. Mark Salter is watching his PropTech business potentially get disrupted and enabled simultaneously. You don't need to spell these implications out. They'll do the math themselves.

### Phase Three of the Arc: Software Creation (The Coding Progression)

*This is the crescendo. You've shown the curve in creative output and business process. Now you show it in raw creation — building things. And the key structural point is that this ISN'T a disconnected story about coding. It's the same exponential arc, hitting a new domain, and accelerating even faster because of the recursive loop.*

> "So that's what the exponential did to creativity. And to business process. Now let me tell you what happened when it hit the thing I never expected — my ability to build."

> "I should tell you — I'm not a developer. I'm an electrical engineer by training who spent twenty years running ad agencies and doing strategy consulting at BCG. I'd never written real code in my life."

> "About a year ago, I built my first piece of software. An order-taking system for the private chef who makes our family meals. Nothing fancy. Worked well. Took me a week."

> "Remember that number. A week."

*"Remember that number" is a flag — the audience knows a payoff is coming.*

**The personal expression bridge — the song:**

*Before you go into the coding acceleration, land the song story here. It belongs in the arc because it's the same curve hitting personal expression — and it sets up the emotional register for the daughter/hot dog cart story later. The point isn't "here's a nice personal anecdote." The point is: the same force that changed images, changed my business, also changed what I could express as a human being.*

> "And around the same time, I had another experience that hit me differently. I'd written a poem for my wife for her birthday. And I love to sing — I consider myself a singing enthusiast."

*"Singing enthusiast" should get a small laugh. Lean into it.*

> "I found a tool that generates music from text. I took my poem and stayed up half the night going back and forth with it. I knew what I wanted the song to sound like — I could hear it in my head — but I'd never had the musical vocabulary to describe it, let alone compose it."

> "I would have been embarrassed trying to explain it to a real musician. But I could keep iterating until it felt right. And last year — for my wife's 50th birthday — I got up in front of a hundred people with a live band and performed that song."

*Pause. Let the room picture it.*

> "Same exponential. Different domain. A year earlier, that was impossible for me — not because I lacked the desire, but because the gap between having the feeling and expressing it required passing through other people who would inevitably filter my intent. That gap closed."

*Now back to the coding acceleration:*

> "Then in December — about two months ago — something shifted in the coding tools. They got to a point where I could move fast enough that the bottleneck was no longer the technology. The bottleneck was how fast I could think."

Now walk through the builds — not as a list, but as an accelerating streak. Each one faster than the last, each one more improbable:

> "I built a full iOS travel management app. From concept to working product — four days. That included learning how to even scope the project."

> "I built an encrypted agent-to-agent communication protocol."

*If you see technical people react — Troy, Benjamin — you can briefly acknowledge: "Yeah, that one was fun."*

> "I built an agentic creator discovery engine for our agency."

> "I built a coloring book generator for my daughter — she sketches something and it turns into printable coloring book pages."

> "And I built Swarmify — a twelve-agent orchestration system. Twelve AI agents working together, coordinated, each with a specific role."

> "One person. Two months. Work that would have required a team of thirty and a year of development time."

*Pause. But don't let the room settle into "that's impressive" mode. Push into the structural insight — the recursive loop and the invention loop. This is the part they haven't heard before.*

> "But here's what's *actually* happening. It's not just that I built these things. It's that each thing I built made me better and faster at building the next thing."

> "I started with a single AI agent helping me code. Then I built the twelve-agent orchestration system. Then I built a generation pipeline so my project scoping follows a specific methodology. Then testing frameworks. Then deployment engines. Then systems that can work autonomously overnight — I go to sleep and wake up to completed work. And then I come back and make the orchestration system itself better."

> "I'm using the technology to improve how I use the technology. And my own learning compounds on top of that. So the speed isn't just fast — it's accelerating."

**The Invention Loop — Pepper and the Knowledge Graph:**

*This is the part that takes it from impressive to genuinely mind-bending. You're not just building faster — you're inventing new things you didn't know existed days earlier.*

> "And here's where it gets truly strange. Let me give you one specific example."

> "Three weeks ago, I didn't know what a knowledge graph was. I mean — I'd heard the term. I didn't understand what it meant architecturally, why it mattered, or how it was different from a regular database."

> "I started exploring because I wanted my personal AI assistant to have real memory — to actually understand context about my life, my projects, my relationships. Not just store files, but *know* things."

> "So I started learning. And within a few days — using AI to learn about AI — I didn't just understand knowledge graphs. I had designed my own variant. A custom knowledge graph architecture with an abstraction layer specific to my use case. Something that, as far as I can tell, doesn't exist anywhere else."

> "I went from not knowing what a thing *is* to inventing a new version of that thing. In three days."

*Pause.*

> "That loop — learn, build, invent, improve, learn again — that's what's actually happening when you engage with these tools deeply. You're not just executing faster. You're *thinking* faster. You're iterating on the concepts themselves and discovering new applications that didn't previously exist."

> "The private chef app took a week. The travel app — a *more complex* project — took four days. And the knowledge graph architecture — something I didn't even know the name of three days before I invented a new version of it."

**[HOLD THE SILENCE]**

*This is the most important pause in the entire talk. Do not fill it. Let the room sit with the implication. Three full seconds of quiet. Some people will shift in their seats. That's the sound of a mental model updating.*

**WHY THIS LANDS FOR THIS ROOM:** Dirk Sebastian watched Ganymed Pharmaceuticals — a company connected to the team that would later build BioNTech — follow a similar compound curve in biotech. He has felt this pattern in his bones. Shane, Jonathan, and Wali think in terms of leverage ratios and force multipliers — they've just heard about a human leverage ratio that compounds recursively. Troy, as a developer, knows exactly how improbable this sounds. Charl, as a polymathic creative, is already thinking about what *he* could build. And the invention loop — going from ignorance to invention in three days — should genuinely stun anyone who has ever tried to learn a new technical discipline. The individual implications vary. The universal reaction is the same: *this is real, this is fast, and it's accelerating.*

## SECTION 3: THE PRICE COLLAPSE
**Target: ~7 minutes | Energy: Analytical, building to big-picture, grounded in their reality**

*You've just finished an emotional and personal crescendo. Now shift to the analytical. This section is for the financial minds — Shane, Wali, Stephen, Patrick, Emma — but the implications touch everyone.*

### The Opening Claim

> "So what does all of this mean in economic terms?"

*Slight pause. Direct eye contact.*

> "The cost of creating anything digital is approaching zero."

### The Art Comparison

**ON SCREEN:** Two pieces of digital art, side by side. One is a famous piece that sold for over a million dollars. The other is something you created in three minutes with AI.

> "One of these took months of labor by a trained artist and sold for over a million dollars. The other one I made in about three minutes."

*Let them look.*

> "Can you tell which is which?"

*Don't wait for answers. Move on.*

> "If you're not an expert — and most buyers aren't — probably not. The craft, the texture, the composition — they're functionally equivalent to most eyes."

> "One cost months of human labor. The other cost a subscription fee and two minutes of prompting."

> "That gap — between months and minutes, between millions and pennies — that's what's happening to everything digital. Images. Video. Music. Software. Written content. All of it."

### The Logical Chain — Knowledge Work

> "But here's where it gets bigger. Because digital costs going to zero isn't just about digital *products*."

*Slow down on this next line. It's the key insight:*

> "Digital work *is* knowledge work. And knowledge work is what sits behind everything else."

*Now make it personal to the room. This should feel conversational, not like you're calling people out — more like you're thinking through implications with friends:*

> "What does it mean for legal work when AI can draft contracts and do case research in minutes?"

*A natural glance toward Eric Kerr — not a spotlight, just an acknowledgment.*

> "For accounting when AI can build and optimize tax structures?"

*Same — a glance toward Emma Miller.*

> "For banking when AI can do credit analysis and risk modeling faster than an analyst team?"

*Patrick Robbins.*

> "For real estate when feasibility studies, market comps, and pro forma models take seconds instead of weeks?"

*Mark Salter, Jonathan Cooper.*

> "I'm not saying those professions are obsolete. Not even close."

*This is important. You're in a room with people whose livelihoods are in these fields. Don't be cavalier.*

> "What I'm saying is: the *deliverable layer* is being commoditized. The contract, the spreadsheet, the analysis, the report — the *thing* that used to take weeks to produce — that's the part approaching zero."

> "The value shifts. It moves to judgment. To relationships. To trust. To the human decisions that surround the work product. The lawyer who understands your business well enough to know which contract clause actually matters. The accountant who knows when the tax strategy is technically legal but practically unwise. The banker who knows when to say yes and when to say wait."

> "The work product is table stakes. The wisdom around it — that's where the value concentrates."

**WHY THIS FRAMING MATTERS:** If you just said "AI will replace knowledge work," half the room would tune out in self-defense. By distinguishing between the deliverable layer and the judgment layer, you let people see themselves on the right side of the shift. That's not just diplomacy — it's accurate. The shift *is* toward human judgment. But the deliverable work that used to fill most of the workday? That's evaporating.

### The Extension to Physical

> "And it doesn't stop at digital."

> "I 3D print things in my house now. Drawer organizers. Kitchen tools. Replacement parts for things that broke. Things that would cost twenty dollars in a store — fully loaded cost, about three bucks. And that's with today's consumer-grade equipment."

> "Humanoid robots are coming. Not science fiction — productized, being deployed in warehouses right now. When construction costs, manufacturing costs, infrastructure costs are being compressed by AI-driven automation and robotics..."

*Trail off slightly. Let them complete the thought.*

### The Energy Argument

> "People push back on this. They say — okay, but everything eventually collapses to the cost of energy. And that's true. Energy is the irreducible input."

> "But here's the thing — the unit cost of producing additional energy is also dropping. Solar costs have plummeted by over 90% in two decades. Yes, there's a short-term demand spike from AI compute and data centers. But structurally, the cost per kilowatt continues to fall. Fusion research is accelerating. Battery storage is getting cheaper."

> "If energy approaches zero — and I'm not saying it will tomorrow, but the curve points that way — then *everything* approaches zero."

### The Exception — Scarcity

> "Now, you can find exceptions."

*Look around the room. Gesture to the window, the mountains.*

> "The best ski chalet in Whistler will always have scarcity value. You can't 3D print a view of Blackcomb. True scarcity — location, time, relationships, singular human talent — that's real and it persists."

> "But for most of what we create, build, and deliver? The cost curve points in one direction."

## SECTION 4: THE PRINCIPLES NAMED
**Target: ~2 minutes | Energy: Calm, grounded, authoritative — a teacher naming what the class just learned**

*This is a structural pause in the talk. You've been building for twenty-five minutes through story, demonstration, and analysis. Now you name the architecture — the four principles that organize everything they've experienced.*

**[PHYSICAL SHIFT — step to a different spot in the room if possible, or simply pause long enough that the audience feels the gear change]**

> "So let me name what you've just experienced."

**ON SCREEN:** Each principle appears one at a time as you say it.

**Principle 1:**

> "You can see the direction, but you can never predict the distance. I was off by a decade on images. I had video roughly right. No matter how hard you calibrate, you'll be wrong about something. And so will I."

**Principle 2:**

> "Every output becomes the input for the next thing. Yesterday's deliverable is today's starting point. The brand strategy that used to take eight weeks is now the raw material I feed into the *actual* work. And then that output becomes tomorrow's input. It never stops."

**Principle 3:**

> "The gap between having an idea and making it real is collapsing. The creative chasm — the thing that used to separate imagination from realization — is being eviscerated. A poem becomes a song. A sketch becomes a coloring book. An idea for an app becomes a working product in four days."

**Principle 4:**

> "And the cost of everything is driving toward zero."

*Pause.*

> "Those four things, taken together, change everything. Not just business. *Everything.*"

*The word "everything" is a bridge to the next section. Don't rush past it. Let the audience wonder what you mean. Then show them.*

## SECTION 5: WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOUR WHOLE LIFE
**Target: ~8 minutes | Energy: Moving from intellectual to deeply personal — the most human section of the talk**

> "So what does 'everything' actually mean?"

*This section cycles through dimensions of life, each one landing a different emotional register. The audience should feel the scope widening with each turn.*

### Business

> "My agency used to be organized around the scarcity of creative production. Specialized teams. Eight-week timelines. Elaborate processes. Entire departments that existed because making things was hard and slow."

> "That entire model has been reinvented. Not in theory — literally, in practice, in the last year. And I'm using the same AI tools to make the reinvention faster. The tools that changed the model are now optimizing the new model."

*Brief. You've already covered the agency in Section 2. This is a callback, not a repeat.*

### Personal Expression

> "I wrote a poem. I turned it into a song. I performed it for my wife with a live band in front of a hundred people."

> "Something that was trapped inside me for years — because I didn't have the musical skill to extract it — is now real. Not because AI made me more creative. I always had the idea. I always had the feeling. AI removed the practical barrier between what I felt and what I could make."

### Family — The Hot Dog Cart

*This is the emotional center of the entire talk. Slow down. Get quiet. This should feel like you're telling a story to friends around a fire, not presenting to a conference.*

> "When I was fourteen, my dad suggested I run a hot dog cart for a summer job. Make some money, learn some business. Practical."

> "But I didn't want to just rent a cart. I wanted to *build* one. I had sketches. I had ideas for what it would look like — something distinctive, something different from every other hot dog cart. I wanted it to be *mine*."

*Small smile.*

> "And my dad laughed. Not cruelly. Lovingly. Practically. Because he was right. If I spent the entire summer building the cart, I'd never sell a single hot dog. The economics didn't work. The practical move was to rent the standard cart and get to work."

> "And I took that lesson to heart. For thirty-five years."

*Let the weight of "thirty-five years" sit.*

> "The creative audacity — the willingness to try to build something that didn't exist yet, to make it exactly the way I saw it in my head — got buried under practicality. Under 'be realistic.' Under 'just get the standard thing and do the business.'"

> "It took me thirty-five years to get that back."

*Pause.*

> "Because now — for the first time — the practical objection doesn't hold. There is almost no idea I come up with where I look at it and think 'I can't do that.' Not because every idea is easy. Not because there are no obstacles. But because there's a realistic path to execution for almost anything I can imagine. The ideas aren't out of reach anymore."

> "The summer I wanted to build a hot dog cart, today I *could* build that cart. I could design it in CAD, get it fabricated, have it be exactly what I sketched on that piece of paper. And I could still sell hot dogs all summer."

### The Daughter

> "And so when I look at my daughter..."

*This transition should be almost involuntary — like the thought naturally flows from the hot dog cart to his daughter. Because it does.*

> "When she sketches something and has an idea — my instinct now is completely different from what I internalized from my dad. Instead of 'be practical,' I say 'let's see if we can do it.'"

> "She sketched characters. I took her drawings and made them real — fully rendered, colorful, alive. She said 'maybe we could turn this into a book.' And now we're working on a book together."

> "She's growing up in a world where creative audacity doesn't carry a practical penalty. Where having an idea and making it real are separated by hours, not years and teams and budgets."

### The Reframe — Everyone Is an Artist

> "Nike says if you have a body, you're an athlete."

*Pause.*

> "I say if you have a brain, you're an artist."

> "Because we can all create now. And we can take risks on our ideas because the cost of being wrong is no longer financial destruction. The worst case for trying something creative is that it doesn't work and you spent a weekend. That's it."

### The Question Everyone Asks

> "People always ask me — how do I prepare my kids for this? What should I teach them? What skills will matter?"

> "And the honest answer is: I don't know exactly. Nobody does. The landscape is changing too fast for anyone to give you a credentialed, specific answer."

> "But I think it's this: keep them human. Let them play. Let them create. Let them connect with other humans. Let them take risks. Foster that sense of total possibility."

> "And when they come to you with a crazy idea — help them try it. Because for the first time in human history, the world can keep up with a child's imagination."

**WHY THIS SECTION WORKS FOR THIS AUDIENCE:** Many people in this room are parents. Dirk and Christiane have children. The operators, the financiers — they all think about the future through the lens of their families. The hot dog cart story gives them a visceral, specific memory to attach the abstract principles to. And the shift from "I internalized practicality" to "my daughter doesn't have to" is a generational story that resonates with anyone who has ever told a child to be realistic.

## SECTION 6: THE OWL
**Target: ~3 minutes | Energy: Quiet, intimate, emotionally resonant**

*This is the most delicate section of the talk. The energy should drop to its lowest point — not somber, but still. Like the room has gotten smaller.*

**[NO SLIDE ON SCREEN — just a blank or very simple background. The focus should be entirely on you and the object in your hand.]**

> "We buy into stories. As humans, that's what we do. It's how we've made sense of the world for a hundred thousand years."

*Reach into your pocket. Pull out the owl figurine. Hold it up so the room can see it, but don't thrust it forward — keep it close to you.*

> "Most of you would look at this and see a little owl. A trinket. If you saw it at a garage sale, you wouldn't give it a second look. Maybe a quarter. Maybe you'd just walk past it."

> "This was my dad's."

*Pause.*

> "He died at forty-nine. I was twenty-three."

*Don't elaborate on the circumstances. Don't ask for sympathy. Just state the fact and let the room hold it.*

> "I pass this in my office every day. And it reminds me of the man he was. The relationship I had with him. The things he taught me — including the hot dog cart lesson."

*The audience will connect this to the earlier story on their own. Do NOT spell out the connection: "And isn't it interesting that the man who told me to be practical never got to see..." — no. The audience will feel it. Trust them.*

> "The value of this owl isn't in what it is. It's in my participation with it. The meaning I bring to it. The story it carries."

> "My business partner Christian has a book coming out in September called 'Wired to Participate.' And the thesis — which I believe in my bones — is that what we value as humans, what we've *always* valued, is our own participation in the creation of meaning."

> "We don't just consume. We participate. We choose. We connect. We tell stories. We imbue things with significance that isn't in the thing itself. That's what makes us human."

> "And in a world where you can have anything — where creation is as plentiful as air, where the cost of making things approaches zero — the value doesn't disappear."

> "It concentrates. In us. In our choices. In our connections. In our stories. In our participation."

*Pause.*

> "AI doesn't threaten that. It amplifies it. Because for the first time in history, nothing stands between your idea and its expression."

*Put the owl back in your pocket. That physical action closes the beat.*

**WHY THIS WORKS:** The owl is a physical anchor — something real and tangible in a talk that's been largely about the digital and abstract. Pulling a physical object out of your pocket in a presentation about AI is unexpected, and that contrast is what makes it powerful. The connection to his father layers the personal narrative (the hot dog cart, the thirty-five years of buried audacity) without being heavy-handed. And the bridge to "Wired to Participate" gives the philosophical frame a commercial anchor — there's a book coming, there's a thesis that's been formalized. This isn't just Amir's feelings. It's a researched, articulated worldview.

## SECTION 7: THE CHOICE
**Target: ~2 minutes | Energy: Rising from quiet to strong and clear — a call to action without being a call to action**

> "People are starting to use words that didn't exist five years ago. Post-scarcity economics. Post-labor markets. Universal basic income for AI-displaced workers."

> "I don't have answers for what the full model looks like. Nobody does. And anyone who tells you they do is selling something."

> "But I can tell you the direction. And I can tell you what I feel."

*This next passage should be the most personal and direct thing you say in the entire talk:*

> "The ability to connect directly to what I'm creating — in the way I always imagined artists do, that feeling of the thing in your head becoming real through your hands — is something I'm experiencing for the first time in my life. And it's happened in the last six months."

> "After twenty-plus years of running businesses and building strategies and making presentations — I'm finally *making things*. And it's changed how I think about everything."

*Beat.*

> "So you have a choice."

> "You can look at everything I've just shown you and feel fear. The cost of everything you sell is driving toward zero. The competitive moats you've built are being eroded. The expertise you've developed over decades can be replicated in minutes by someone with the right tools."

> "That's all true."

*Don't dismiss the fear. Acknowledge it.*

> "Or you can look at it the way I do."

> "This is the most creative, most exciting, most opportunity-rich moment in the history of business. In the history of *being a person.* One individual can now do what thirty people used to do. The gap between having an idea and making it real has never been smaller. And the value of being human — of participating, of choosing, of creating meaning — has never been higher."

> "The question isn't whether this is happening. It's who you want to be while it does."

**[FULL STOP]**

*Do not say "thank you." Do not say "so yeah." Do not say "I'll open it up for questions." Just stop talking. Hold eye contact with the room. Let the silence do the work.*

*After three to five seconds of silence, the room will respond — applause, a murmur, someone will ask the first question. If there's a moderator, they'll step in. If not, you can simply say:*

> "I'd love to hear what's on your mind."

*And then take questions.*

## NOTES ON QUESTIONS & DISCUSSION

The Q&A will likely run 10-15 minutes and will naturally emerge from the talk. A few likely threads and how to handle them:

**"How do I actually start?"** — Be generous and specific. Recommend tools by name. Offer to help after the session. This is where the relationship deepens.

**"What about regulation / ethics / job loss?"** — Don't dismiss these. Acknowledge the complexity. You can say something like: "These are real questions and I don't have clean answers. What I do know is that the technology doesn't wait for the regulation. So the question becomes: do you want to be navigating this from a position of understanding, or a position of catching up?"

**"How does this apply to my specific business?"** — This is where the personalized companion app pays off. You can literally say: "Check your phone — there might be something in there about that." Or you can riff on the specific business. You know these people. Use what you know.

**"Aren't you worried about [specific AI risk]?"** — Be honest about what worries you and what doesn't. Nuance is the credibility play here. Anyone can be a cheerleader. The depth comes from showing you've wrestled with the hard questions.
