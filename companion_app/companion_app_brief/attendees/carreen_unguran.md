## How AI & Exponential Technology Impacts Their World

For a telecom-regulatory/CRTC-facing practitioner (and for BC government regulatory/permitting leadership), AI and exponential technologies primarily change (1) the speed and complexity of regulatory change, (2) the evidentiary/record-building workload, and (3) the risk posture around privacy, security, and trust.

1) Faster, more complex regulatory environment
- AI adoption in telecom networks (and related standards activity) increases the volume of technical claims that must be evaluated for reasonableness, security, and consumer impact. ([ISED principles on AI adoption in telecom](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/ised/en/principles-artificial-intelligence-adoption-telecommunications-industry))

2) AI affects the sectors CRTC regulates (even if the CRTC is not an AI regulator)
- Canadian regulators have explicitly noted that even if the CRTC does not regulate AI systems directly, AI and synthetic media can materially affect communications services and industries the CRTC regulates (broadcasting, news, accessibility, etc.), implying continuous monitoring and policy adaptation. ([Canadian Digital Regulators Forum report](https://competition-bureau.canada.ca/en/how-we-foster-competition/education-and-outreach/publications/canadian-digital-regulators-forum-synthetic-media-digital-landscape))

3) Public-record intensity + quasi-judicial process creates a “document industrial complex” ripe for AI augmentation
- CRTC proceedings rely heavily on written submissions, interrogatories, procedural letters, and voluminous records; AI can compress and structure evidence, but must preserve citation and traceability for defensibility in hearings and possible appeals. ([CRTC archived letter 2021-08-19](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/archive/2021/lt210819a.htm))

4) Governance and trust requirements become central
- “Agentic AI” (systems that can plan/execute tasks within boundaries) is increasingly framed as relevant to government functions, but requires strong governance, data foundations, and auditability—directly aligned to the expectations placed on public-sector decision-making and regulated entities. ([IDC on agentic AI in government](https://www.idc.com/resource-center/blog/preparing-government-for-agentic-ai/))

Key challenges AI could address in her work
- Horizon scanning: triaging regulatory updates, consultation notices, procedural deadlines, and stakeholder filings at scale.
- Record synthesis: summarizing multi-party submissions, extracting disputed facts, and mapping arguments to statutory tests.
- Drafting support: producing first-pass versions of interrogatories, evidence tables, argument outlines, and response matrices while preserving citations.
- Technical-to-policy translation: converting network/engineering AI claims into “regulatory language” (consumer outcomes, competition impacts, privacy/security).

Key opportunities she should be aware of
- AI-enabled regulatory intelligence and obligations mapping can materially reduce cycle time from “new decision/notice” to “actionable internal plan,” which is a competitive advantage for regulated entities and a capacity multiplier for government teams.
- AI governance competence (policy + implementation controls) is becoming a differentiator as governments and regulators scrutinize AI use across critical infrastructure and public services. ([Government of Canada AI Register announcement](https://www.canada.ca/en/treasury-board-secretariat/news/2025/11/canada-launches-first-register-of-ai-uses-in-federal-government.html))

## Personalized AI Resource Guide

### AI Tools Recommended

Regulatory/policy-focused
- Microsoft Copilot (M365): accelerates drafting, meeting notes, and synthesis in Word/Outlook/Teams workflows typical of regulatory affairs and government work; best used with strict “cite-to-source” and confidentiality rules.
- Claude / ChatGPT / Gemini: strong for first-pass summarization of long consultation records, argument mapping, and generating structured response matrices (always require human review and source citation).

Regulatory intelligence / change management
- Compliance.ai: monitoring and organizing regulatory updates with targeted alerts and enriched tagging—useful model even if telecom coverage must be adapted to Canadian telecom sources. ([Compliance.ai regulatory intelligence](https://www.compliance.ai/solution/regulatory-intelligence/))
- Regology: positions itself as an AI-driven regulatory intelligence platform that can identify applicable regulatory content, monitor changes, and generate obligations/risks/controls/policies. ([Regology](https://regology.com))

Evidence management + defensible workflows
- Stack AI (governed workflow automation): example of using governed AI workflows to standardize compliance evidence and reporting (conceptually relevant to telecom regulatory compliance automation). ([Stack AI telecom compliance guide](https://www.stack-ai.com/insights/automating-compliance-for-telecommunications-companies-a-complete-guide-to-telecom-regulatory-compliance-automation-with-stackai))

Practical ‘must-have’ utilities
- Otter.ai / Descript: fast transcription + summaries for stakeholder interviews, consultations, and internal briefings (tools listed publicly as used in an AI policy context, indicating mainstream adoption). ([Canadian Affairs AI policy](https://www.canadianaffairs.news/ai-policy/))

### Agentic Frameworks & Apps

Workflow automation & agent orchestration (practical)
- Microsoft Power Automate + Copilot Studio: build semi-autonomous workflows (intake → triage → draft → route for approval) for regulatory correspondence, consultation monitoring, and internal approvals.
- Zapier / Make: lightweight automation for alerts (CRTC notices/RSS, keyword triggers, stakeholder updates) into Teams/Slack/email digests.

Multi-agent / developer frameworks (for internal teams or vendors)
- LangGraph (LangChain): for building multi-step, stateful agents (e.g., “CRTC record analyzer” that extracts issues → builds citation map → drafts outline).
- Microsoft Semantic Kernel: orchestration layer to connect LLM prompts with tools/data and enforce governance boundaries.

Government-grade perspective on agentic AI
- Agentic AI is explicitly framed as valuable in government for operational orchestration and policy/decision support, but only when paired with structured data and auditability—important guardrails for any ‘autonomous’ system used in high-stakes regulatory work. ([IDC on agentic AI in government](https://www.idc.com/resource-center/blog/preparing-government-for-agentic-ai/))

### Key Aspects of Their Role Being Transformed by AI

For a telecom regulatory advisor / government regulatory & permitting leader, AI is transforming:

1) Regulatory horizon scanning
- Automated collection of new/changed policies, staff letters, consultation notices, and multi-party submissions; AI-assisted triage to identify “must-respond” items and deadlines. ([CRTC Regulatory Policies 2025 index](https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/8045/p2025.htm))

2) Record digestion and issue mapping
- Summarizing large dockets, clustering stakeholder arguments, extracting contested facts, and producing “issue trees” for leadership.

3) Drafting and redlining
- Faster first drafts of interventions, information requests, briefing notes, and decision summaries; automated redlines across versions of filings.

4) Evidence traceability (new minimum standard)
- Increased expectation that any AI-assisted synthesis is auditable with citations to the record, because regulatory processes are quasi-judicial and decisions are challengeable.

5) Technical-regulatory translation
- Translating AI/ML-in-network topics (privacy/security, bias, model assurance, incident reporting) into compliance language aligned to Canadian expectations for safe, secure, trustworthy AI integration in telecom. ([ISED principles on AI adoption in telecom](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/ised/en/principles-artificial-intelligence-adoption-telecommunications-industry))

6) Policy design under uncertainty
- Scenario modeling for how new AI-driven products (AI customer service, AI network optimization, synthetic media) interact with broadcasting/news rules, accessibility, consumer protection, and competition. ([Canadian Digital Regulators Forum report](https://competition-bureau.canada.ca/en/how-we-foster-competition/education-and-outreach/publications/canadian-digital-regulators-forum-synthetic-media-digital-landscape))

Telecom operator AI adoption (relevant to CRTC-facing roles)
- Canadian telecom-related AI adoption discussions emphasize privacy and secure integration of AI into telecom operations, reflecting the direction of expectations likely to be relevant in regulatory dialogues and risk management. ([ISED principles on AI adoption in telecom](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/ised/en/principles-artificial-intelligence-adoption-telecommunications-industry))
